# AI Incident Reasoning Limits

Guardrails for AI-generated outputs across all workflows in `06-ai-workflows/`.

> AI in this system is allowed to suggest, group, infer, and summarize — but never to decide, finalize, or authorize anything that affects production systems, customers, or incident conclusions without human confirmation.


## Purpose

Incident response depends on accuracy under pressure, not confidence under uncertainty.

AI systems are useful for accelerating analysis, but they can:

* over-generalize patterns
* hallucinate missing context
* compress ambiguity into false certainty

This file defines strict boundaries to ensure AI output remains advisory, traceable, and reviewable.


## Hard Prohibitions (Non-Negotiable)

AI outputs must NEVER:

### 1. Declare Final Truth

* No final root cause determination
* No definitive statements of impact origin
* No “this is the issue” framing without explicit human confirmation

### 2. Execute or Approve Actions

* No production changes (rollback, delete, restart, scale, disable)
* No customer communication approval
* No incident closure decisions

### 3. Fabricate or Fill Missing Data

* Never guess timestamps, services, metrics, or log content
* Never interpolate missing timeline segments
* Never “complete” partial data based on plausibility

### 4. Collapse Unverified Narratives

* Do not merge incidents or failure streams unless explicitly supported by evidence
* Do not unify multiple hypotheses into a single assumed storyline


## Required Output Discipline

Every AI-generated output in this folder must:

* Be traceable to explicit input data (logs, metrics, alerts, tickets, or prior workflow outputs)
* Separate facts from interpretation
* Avoid mixing inference and observation in the same statement
* Include a confidence label (see below)

If a claim cannot be tied to a source signal, it must be marked as:

> Unverified / Insufficient evidence


## Hallucination Risk Modes

AI incident reasoning commonly fails in predictable ways:

### 1. False Precision

Inventing:

* exact timestamps
* service names
* error counts
* sequence order

### 2. Confidence Inflation

Turning probabilistic patterns into definitive statements:

* Incorrect: “DB failures caused the outage”
* Correct: “Pattern is consistent with DB-related degradation”

### 3. Silent Context Completion

Filling missing data with plausible assumptions instead of marking gaps.

### 4. Cross-Incident Contamination

Reusing patterns from prior incidents that are not actually applicable.

**Rule:** Similarity is not evidence.


## Escalation Triggers (Immediate Human Handoff)

Stop AI-driven progression and escalate when:

* Competing AI hypotheses cannot be reconciled with available evidence
* Any risk involves:

  * data loss
  * security exposure
  * compliance breach
* Incident is rapidly cascading or expanding across services
* Confidence is predominantly Low across outputs
* Customer-facing messaging is being prepared or modified
* Multiple workflows disagree on core interpretation of the same signals


## Confidence Labeling Standard

Every AI output must include one of:

### High Confidence

* Direct, multi-source evidence supports the conclusion
* No significant contradictions present

### Medium Confidence

* Partial evidence or indirect correlation
* Some missing context or minor ambiguity

### Low Confidence

* Weak signal strength
* Single-source inference
* Pattern-based suggestion without confirmation

**Important:**
Confidence describes evidence strength, not urgency or usefulness.

Low confidence findings may still be operationally valuable.


## Required Disclaimer Block

All AI-generated summaries, hypotheses, or recommendations must include:

```
This output was generated by an AI-assisted incident workflow using
available signals at the time. It is not verified. Treat all conclusions
as hypotheses and confirm through direct investigation before taking action.
```


## Output Integrity Rules

* Facts and interpretations must be visually or structurally separable
* No blending of “observed” and “assumed” statements
* No incident resolution language unless human-confirmed
* All unknowns must be explicitly marked


## Quality Checklist

Before any AI output is accepted:

* Every claim is traceable to a source signal
* No assumptions are presented as facts
* Confidence label is present and consistent
* No action or decision authority is implied
* Escalation conditions are evaluated
* Customer-facing content is not finalized


## Key Principle

AI accelerates understanding — it does not replace judgment.

When evidence is incomplete, the correct behavior is not to guess faster, but to narrow uncertainty and surface it clearly.
